What Makati lost and Taguig gained

The Supreme Court settled a decades-long dispute between two Metro Manila cities. Only one came out as the winner.

By Prinz Magtulis

What Makati lost and Taguig gained

By Prinz Magtulis


I

f Makati Mayor Abigail Binay decides to run for reelection next year, a big chunk of residents who elected her will no longer see her name in the ballot.
That is after the Supreme Court last year reclassified 10 barangays originally under Makati City as now part of its neighboring Taguig City. The court decision, which put an end to decades-long territorial dispute between the two cities, left Makati at the losing end, decimating almost its entire second congressional district and putting into question how the city will be governed.
The transition has already started: previous Makati regional trial courts have been transferred to Taguig's jurisdiction, Makati has started budgeting for next fiscal year without accounting for revenues from the 10 barangays and it is losing, and even public requests to government agencies for "Makati data" would exclude the territories subject of the ruling.

At the heart of the decision is the transfer of the “embo” barangays namely Cembo, Comembo, East Rembo, Pitogo, Pembo, West Rembo and South Cembo. In addition, barangays Rizal, Post Proper Northside and Post Proper Southside were also moved to Taguig. Fort Bonifacio, which has typically been under Taguig, would stay with the city under the same high court ruling.
These 10 barangays are considered vote-rich areas. During the October 2023 barangay elections, the barangays collectively had over 212,000 registered voters, data from the Commission on Elections showed. That represented over 45% of total voters for that period. In 2022, Binay, who said she is considering vying for Taguig mayor post next year, won a third term as Makati mayor with 338,819 votes.
The impact goes beyond local politics. The 10 barangays are grouped under Makati's second congressional district, together with barangays Guadalupe Nuevo, Guadalupe Viejo and Pinagsamahan. This district is currently served by Rep. Luis Jose Campos. The transfer means only two barangays will be left under that district with a cumulative population of just over 33,000 as of May 2020, which would be the lowest after Batanes's lone district. The fate of the district remains to be seen.
That, in turn, makes the second congressional district of Makati the second smallest in terms of population, next only to the lone congressional district of Batanes.

The geographical change also has bearing on public funds. By law, 20% of national government revenues are shared with the barangays, which count that as part of their income. These revenues, in turn, go toward financing public projects such as infrastructure and providing social services to their residents.

However, it is difficult to draw conclusions from these numbers, which also do not reflect efficiency in disposing of cases. Case backlogs are an enduring problem in the broader judiciary, and the Supreme Court has made efforts to speed up the resolution of cases, both within its own backyard and in lower courts where it exercises administrative powers.
By tenure, the longest sitting justice to date is Senior Associate Justice Marvic Leonen, who was appointed by the late president Benigno Aquino III, in November 2012. That makes Leonen the ponente of over 800 decisions in court for the past decade, the most among sitting justices, according to court data.
Controlling for the number of months they have occupied their chambers so far, Leonen places third behind Associate Justices Henri Jean Paul Inting and Ramon Paul Hernando on the average number of penned decisions promulgated every month, our analysis showed.
On average, the justices wrote about four decisions a month, data showed.
